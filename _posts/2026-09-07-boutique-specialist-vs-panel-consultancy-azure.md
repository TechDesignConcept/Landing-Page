# Boutique Specialist vs Panel Consultancy: Choosing Azure Delivery Help

We are a boutique, so read this with that declared. The purpose here is not to argue that boutiques are always the answer; they are not, and the framework below says so explicitly. The purpose is to give you the decision logic that procurement processes tend to skip, because "who is on the panel" and "who is right for this work" are different questions.

## The three buying options, honestly

**Panel incumbents and licensing-led firms** are the right call when the engagement is volume: hundreds of seats to migrate, licensing to administer, a national footprint to service, or a contractual need for a large firm's balance sheet behind the SLA. They win on capacity and coverage. The trade-off is that platform engineering depth is unevenly distributed across large benches, and you do not get to choose which part of the bench arrives.

**Big 4 and strategy-led firms** are the right call when the problem is organisational rather than technical: a transformation programme needing board-level authority, multi-vendor orchestration, or air cover for hard decisions. The trade-off is delivery distance: the partner who sold the vision is several layers above the analyst writing your Bicep, if anyone is writing Bicep at all.

**Boutique specialists** are the right call when the work is deep and specific: a landing zone that has to survive an audit, a security programme in a regulated estate, a governance baseline expressed as code, an AI platform with a defensible control story. You are buying a named senior practitioner's judgment applied directly to your problem. The trade-offs are real: capacity ceilings, key-person concentration, and no bench to absorb a surge.

## The questions that expose the difference

Whoever you are evaluating, these questions sort marketing from capability quickly:

1. **"Who exactly will do the work, and can we interview them?"** If the answer is a role title rather than a person, price in the pyramid.
2. **"Show us a policy baseline or module library from a previous engagement."** Redacted is fine. An engineering-led firm has artefacts; a slideware-led firm has frameworks.
3. **"What do we own at handover, and what will we still need you for?"** The honest answer includes a repository, a runbook, and a knowledge transfer session. Dependency-by-design shows up in this answer.
4. **"What is out of scope?"** Specialists answer instantly, because a sharp scope is how they survive. Vague scope boundaries predict change-request economics later.
5. **"What have you shipped in environments like ours?"** Metrics and durations, not logos. A 12-month security programme with measurable coverage beats a wall of brand names.

## The dishonest comparisons to avoid

Boutiques comparing themselves to large firms usually cheat by comparing their principal to the other firm's graduate. Large firms cheat in mirror image, comparing their national capacity to a boutique's headcount as if every engagement needs two hundred people. Both framings dodge the real question: what does this specific engagement need, depth or breadth?

A useful heuristic: if the statement of work could be delivered by three senior engineers or fewer, buying a large firm means paying for coordination you do not need. If it genuinely needs twenty people, a boutique either declines it or becomes the risk.
